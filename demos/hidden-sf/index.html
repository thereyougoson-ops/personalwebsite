The bargain · since 1985

You paid for these with the skyline.

When San Francisco let downtown grow taller, it attached a condition: build up, and you owe the public somewhere to sit down.

01 — The deal

The 1985 Downtown Plan reshaped the rules for the C-3 district. Developers could add height and floor area — but in exchange they had to carve out open space the public could actually use.

02 — Open space for height

The rate was blunt: one square foot of public open space for every fifty square feet of office. A tower's plaza, atrium, or rooftop terrace became part of the price of building it — written into Planning Code Section 138.

03 — Ten flavors of public

The result wasn't one kind of space but ten: plazas and urban gardens at street level, sun terraces and view terraces on the roof, atriums and greenhouses indoors, and "snippets" — slivers too small to be anything else.

04 — Hidden by design

Since 2012 the city has required a standardized plaque at every entrance, meant to steer you up to the rooftops and into the atriums. Many still fall short — SPUR called signage “the most glaring defect” in the whole program. The result is a network of public rooms that read as private. Which is exactly why this map exists.

The ten types

Ten ways to be a public space.

The code doesn't just say “open space.” It names ten kinds — from a street-level plaza to a glassed-in greenhouse fourteen floors up. The official taxonomy, with how many of each we have.

▢ Plaza
An open-air, mostly paved space at street level, furnished with seating to draw people off the sidewalk.
❧ Urban Garden
A small, sheltered landscaped pocket — defined by planters, sitting ledges, and a fountain.
▪ Snippet
A small, sunny sitting area: a few seats, minimal landscaping. The leftover slivers.
◳ Indoor Park
An interior room open to the public, with at least one full-glass wall facing the street.
⇄ Pedestrian Walkway
A public sitting area threaded through a galleria, arcade, alley, or pedestrian mall.
☀ Sun Terrace
A wind-sheltered perch on an upper floor, placed to catch the midday sun.
◈ Atrium
A skylit interior court — glass-covered, open to the public, with seating below.
❋ Greenhouse
A glassed-in enclosure — at least 1,000 sq ft under a 20-foot ceiling — full of light and plantings.
❦ Urban Park
A larger green space led by trees, lawn, and water rather than pavement.
◹ View Terrace
Like a sun terrace, but sited for the city view instead of the sun.

Built over sixty years

A surge after the Plan.

Nearly half of these spaces predate the rules. Then 1985 changed the deal — and the gift itself: the 1980s handed over plazas open around the clock, while the 2000s built nearly as many that lock when the lobby does.

How this was made

The method, and the caveats.

The data

Every space here comes from the City's own inventory of Privately-Owned Public Open Space, published by the San Francisco Planning Department on DataSF. The spaces themselves are required under Planning Code Section 138; SPUR's 2009 survey first catalogued and critiqued them.

Dataset 65ik-7wqd · DataSF

The "obscurity" score

A space scores higher when it's harder for the public to find or use: an indirect entrance through a private lobby, poor or missing signage, limited hours, or an interior location. It's a reading of the data, not an official rating.

Read this carefully

The descriptions, hours, and amenities are the city's own field notes — some are years old and a few are frank about uncertainty. One space is listed as under construction; another as having disputed public access. We kept them in, and labeled them.
